This website is about addressing the growing problem of SMS spam and scam messages in the Philippines. Anchored on SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure), it explores how machine learning and technological innovation can strengthen digital infrastructure and protect users from fraud. It also connects to SDG 16 (Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions) by promoting safer communication channels and consumer trust.

Overview

While mobile communication in the Philippines has made connectivity more accessible, it has also become a tool for cybercriminals. The rise of SMS scams — from fake promos to phishing schemes — threatens public trust in digital infrastructure . Despite government and telecom crackdowns, fraudulent texts continue to evolve, highlighting the need for smarter, data-driven solutions.

This is why, we, the 1digit, chose to focus on SMS spam and fraud detection: to explore how machine learning can strengthen infrastructure and protect Filipino consumers.

(PrivadoVPN, 2022)

Problem

SMS scams in the Philippines are widespread and constantly adapting, making it difficult for existing defenses to keep up. This undermines consumer trust in mobile communication and financial services.

Solution

Our solution is to apply machine learning models using both classical and transformer to detect and analyze scam texts. By studying linguistic patterns unique to Philippine SMS, we aim to develop a robust detection system that enhances trust in mobile infrastructure and protects users from fraud.

Background

The rapid growth of mobile communication in the Philippines has been accompanied by an alarming rise in spam and scam text messages. In 2024, over 6.1 million SMS scams were reported nationwide, with the final quarter contributing almost one-third of incidents (Dela Cruz, 2025). Although the Department of Information and Communications Technology (DICT) and the National Telecommunications Commission (NTC) have intensified crackdowns to block billions of fraudulent SMS and dismantle scam hubs, cybercriminals have continued to adapt, often using spoofing and phishing tactics that weaken consumer trust in mobile infrastructure (Salonga, 2024).

Telecommunications companies have also scaled up defenses. In 2025, Globe Telecom launched the SMS Scam Shield, an in-app feature designed to automatically flag suspicious texts. Globe also reported blocking over 484 million scam and spam messages in the first half of 2025 alone (Amojelar, 2025; Camus, 2025). These interventions highlight industry recognition of the scale of the problem, yet they also underscore the need for complementary, data-driven solutions that can generalize across new and evolving scam strategies.

Globally, text-based fraud remains a pressing concern. The FBI recently issued warnings about “smishing” attacks targeting mobile users (Zilber, 2025), while researchers have reported the growing use of SMS “blasters” that enable mass scam campaigns (Wired, 2025). In the Philippines, recent law enforcement operations dismantled hubs employing hundreds in cybercrime activities (Associated Press, 2025). Against this, developing robust machine learning models for SMS spam and fraud detection is both timely and aligned with SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure) and SDG 16 (Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions) to strengthen trust in digital communication while addressing cybercrime.

Research Questions

This left us pondering the following...

1. Which machine learning approach (classical ML models vs. transformer-based models) achieves better performance in detecting SMS spam/scam messages in the Philippines?

Null Hypothesis:

There is no significant difference in classification performance (accuracy, F1-score) between classical ML models and transformer-based models in detecting SMS spam/scam.

Alternative Hypothesis:

Transformer-based models significantly outperform classical ML models in SMS spam/scam detection.

2. What are the common characteristics and linguistic patterns of SMS spam/scam messages in the Philippines, and how do they differ from non-spam (ham) messages?

Null Hypothesis:

There are no significant differences in linguistic patterns (e.g., message length, keyword frequencies) between spam/scam and non-spam messages.

Alternative Hypothesis:

Spam/scam messages exhibit distinct linguistic patterns compared to non-spam messages.

The following are the datasets contained in the data repository:

  • 1. 🇵🇭 Philippine Spam/ Scam SMS by BwandoWando

    This data set contains SMS messages whose contents are considered to be spam and/or are scams. The data was collected by the author from their own cellphone device. The data collected spans from January 23, 2018 to August 27, 2025. The data included in the set contains information such as the name of the sender (or a partially revealed phone number if unavailable), a hashed value of the actual cellphone number, the date and time when it was received, the text contents and the telco company that the cellphone number originates from, if available. The data set has a size of 1014 rows, and is contained in a .csv file.

    • Author: BwandoWando (2024)
    • Time span: Jan 23, 2018 – Aug 27, 2025
    • Size: 1,014 rows (.csv file)
    • Includes: sender info (masked), date/time, message content, telco carrier (if available), spam/scam label
    • Bwandowando. (2024). Philippine spam/scam SMS [Dataset]. Kaggle. https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/bwandowando/philippine-spam-sms-messages

  • 2. Tagalog-SMS by zero

    This data set contains SMS messages received by the author from their cellphone device. The data collected spans from April 30, 2024 to April 13, 2025. The data included in the set contains information such as the name (or number) of the sender, the date and time when it was received, the text contents, and the category of the message received. The messages are categorized as spam, advertisements, government messages, notifications, and OTPs (one-time-password). The data set has a size of 3454 rows, and is contained in a spreadsheet file.

Methodology

1. Modeling (RQ1 – SDG 9)

  • Classical models: Logistic Regression, Random Forest (using scikit-learn).
  • Transformer baseline: Use pre-trained models (e.g., BERT or DistilBERT) either in inference-only mode or with light fine-tuning.
  • Compare performance with Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score.

2. Statistical Analysis (RQ2 – SDG 9 + 16)

  • Descriptive analysis: Compare message lengths, word counts, and language usage (Tagalog, English, code-switched) between spam and ham.
  • Keyword frequency: Generate frequency tables and word clouds to identify common spam terms.
  • Statistical testing: Apply Chi-square tests to see if certain words/phrases appear disproportionately in spam vs. ham.
  • Visualization: Use bar charts/word clouds to highlight differences in patterns.
